## Abstract

## Background

Food security, essential to public health and welfare, is critically
compromised in the Gaza Strip @unrwa. Long term food supply has been
reduced to a level well below self-sufficiency to meet the needs of the
2.4 million population. The Gaza Strip has been under continuous Israeli
blockade since 2007, while 96% cropland is now damaged or inaccessible
@]. Food shortages substantially worsened since October 2023. Over
2025, the Gaza Strip saw total (March to May) and extensive (May
onwards) restrictions on trucking, and the imposition on Gazans of the
inhumane and inconsistent Gaza Humanitarian Foundation distribution
system. This has reduced overall availability and accessibility of
adequate nutrition.

Evidence of changing nutrition status can support both action and
accountability. Estimates from the first semester of the war in Gaza saw
substantial caloric deficits relative to healthy diet requirements
@. … A sequence of Integrated Phase Classification (IPC) reports culminated in a formal designation of famine… However, there remains little formal and consistent evidence of nutritional status over time.

In July 2025, we initiated a project to monitor the nutritional status
of humanitarian staff inside the Gaza Strip. The project aimed to inform
humanitarian decision-making, advocacy and diplomatic action. We used
simple, low-cost methods to enable staff to self-report their weight
over time.

Our specific objectives were to:

1.  Estimate average weight and body-mass index (BMI) change since
    pre-war (7 October 2023) levels;
2.  Monitor the relative and absolute evolution of weight and BMI over
    time;
3.  Describe differences in weight and BMI change by sex, age,
    governorate of residence, and responsibility for child nutrition

## Methods

*take from[protocol](./attic/protocol.docx)*

We collaborated with Save the Children International and United Nations
Relief and Works Agency for Palestinian Refugees in the Near East
(UNRWA) to set up an information system to track weight measurements
among staff. These organisations provide humanitarian services inside
the Gaza Strip.

All participation from those providing data, resources, support, and
analysis, was entirely voluntary.

We automated analysis and visualisations as self-readings were reported.
We excluded observations outside of plausible ranges (weight, 30-180kg;
BMI: 10-60; daily rate of change in weight since study entry over
+/-10%; age, 16-99 years; dependent children, 0-20).

## Discussion

We provided real-time information on the nutritional status of
humanitarian staff in the Gaza Strip from July 2025, with the aim to
inform humanitarian decision-making, advocacy, and diplomatic action.
Data were anonymised and aggregated, with visualisation of trends over
time and space to illustrate conditions inside Gaza, available on a
public website. We found …

We used a rapid, low-cost study design to add information to a
data-sparse crisis. This aimed to inform humanitarian decision-making,
advocacy, and diplomatic action. We relied on voluntary participation
among humanitarian aid staff, a group likely unrepresentative of the
general population @cite]. Participation depended on capacity,
advocacy, and interest, and we noted relatively few participants
providing follow-up measurements.

Weight measurements have been used to establish famine classifications,
and in Gaza particularly among children. Adults are typically not
represented in these samples, and there has been little consistent
follow up of the same participants over time. This has made it difficult
to assess whether changes are due to sampling differences or individual
nutritional status over time. In comparison with similar work…

In this study, we used weight as an indicator of the nutritional and
food security situation. However, weight is only a proxy for documenting
nutritional status over time @burchiFoodAvailabilityNutritional2016. Famines have multidimensional causes
and consequences @sen. Other indicators could also include morbidity
(e.g. admissions or consultations for conditions related to acute
malnutrition, such as pneumonia and acute diarrhoea); relapses from
malnutrition treatment, the rate of which typically indicates
insufficient food at household level; incidence of from infections
following trauma injuries, common in undernourished individuals; food
availability in markets; and food distribution data. …citations

We worked collaboratively across institutions to design and implement
this work, with technical support in the UK led by requests from
humanitarian aid organisations in Gaza. At the same time, research
across populations is both inefficient and unequal. We were unable to
assess the provide continuous maintenance that is essential for
sustainable software @SSI. This can be mitigated with core principles of
equitable research partnerships: fairness, respect, honesty, and care
@TRUST. We believe it is possible for such work to empower civil society
to tell its own story, not only through media, but also by supporting
the systematic capture of contemporary experience.

Unpredictable crises should be met with predictable situational
awareness to enable short term response and long term accountability. We
believe there is disproportionate value of information to be gained from
simple and collaborative data collection. Unconventional populations and
methods, e.g. through humanitarian assistance work, can still provide
public health indicators that are both available and timely
@checchi2017]. Such data can and should be made available routinely,
e.g. on the United Nations data repository, HDX. Consistency is
essential, and requires shifting from intermittent reports to automated
data pipelines and live visualisations. Equally importantly, sustained
data collation creates a documented trace of contemporary experience.
Ultimately, this adds to the weight of evidence from which to anchor
historical accountability.
